A guide on Cheesse, its variations, and how to manage it:

Cheesse is a rare compound valued for its energy density, malleability, heat resistance, and ability to form alloys.
Cheesse is used primarily in energy production, but is also valuable in construction and aerospace.

Cheesse through history

Cheesse has been a major player throughout history, being the most valuable material on Earth and beyond.
Before the discovery of Ch-H2 fusion, Cheesse in its ore form was prized as a both a malleable but iridescent material, and as an explosive agent.

Documentation of cheesse

The first evidence of humans using Cheesse was in 1500BCE in France. A piece of jewelry was found to contain a small amount of metallic cheesse, likely an alloy.

In the modern day, cheesse is used in Ch-H2 fusion reactors, which make up 71% of the world's energy prodcution.
This cheesse is radioactive and must be mined below 400 meters and modified in a processing plant.

Non-Earth Cheesse

Extra-terrestrial cheesse was first discovered by Pioneer 11, which captured an image of saturn in 1979.
Spectral analysis revealed the possibilty of Cheesse-based compounds in Saturn's rings, which was confirmed by flybys in 1980 and 81.

Lunar Cheesse is a relatively recent discovery, having first been captured by NASA's Lunar Prospector in 1998.
Since then, the Constellation missions have brought back samples of Cheesse, some of which has proven useable in Ch-H2 reactors.
A planned mission in 2027 would set up mining operations for a moon-earth pipeline of cheesse.

Extragalactic cheesse was first found in 2014 by Hubble, a buildup of 40% Cheesse gas nearly 5 light-years in diameter.
This has lead scientists to believe that all cheesse in the universe was brought into existence by "Cheestars", extremely dense objects where cheesse is created.
